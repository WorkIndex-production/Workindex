Fact check

Accuracy notes before you act

  • Wallet-to-wallet transfers between accounts owned by the same individual are not taxable events, but they require strict cost basis tracking for subsequent sales.
  • Peer-to-peer (P2P) crypto buyers have a statutory obligation u/s 194S to deduct 1% TDS on transactions exceeding ₹10,000 (or ₹50,000 for specified persons).
  • Losses resulting from exchange hacks or lost private keys are not deductible as business or capital losses under Section 115BBH of the Income Tax Act.
  • Crypto received as salary is taxed as a perquisite at fair market value on the date of receipt, and its subsequent sale triggers 30% tax on any further gains.

Questions People Ask

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is a wallet-to-wallet transfer taxable under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty?

No. Moving crypto between wallets owned by the same person is not a taxable transfer. However, cost basis records must be maintained under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty.

2. Who is liable for TDS in P2P crypto transactions?

In P2P transactions, the buyer is legally obligated u/s 194S to deduct 1% TDS on payment to the seller if it exceeds threshold limits under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty.

3. Can I claim tax deductions for crypto lost in a hack?

No. Losses from exchange hacks, theft, or lost private keys cannot be claimed as tax deductions or offset against other crypto gains u/s 115BBH under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty.

4. How is crypto received as salary taxed?

It is taxed as a perquisite at FMV on the date of receipt under Section 17(2). Subsequent sales are subject to the standard 30% crypto capital gains tax under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty.

5. Are stablecoins exempt from the 30% crypto tax?

No. Stablecoins like USDT, USDC, and DAI are classified as virtual digital assets (VDAs) and are taxed at a flat 30% rate on all gains under Crypto P2P Trading Tds 194S Buyer Duty.
